Why your platform's native search engine isn't enough

Shopify, PrestaShop, and WooCommerce index your catalog correctly. The problem isn't indexing — it's everything that happens between the question your visitor types and the results they get.

Most e-commerce sites set up their search engine once, at launch, and never touch it again. That's understandable: internal search doesn't get watched the way homepage bounce rate or checkout flow does. Yet it's often where a meaningful share of lost sales happens.

Here are the three blind spots that come up most often when auditing a native engine.

1. No tolerance for typos

A visitor who types "swetaer" instead of "sweater" gets a blank page in most cases. The engine looks for an exact match, doesn't find one, and stops there. On mobile, where typos are statistically more common, the impact is even more pronounced.

The fix isn't technically complex: configure a tolerance of one to two characters on search terms. But it's almost never enabled by default.

2. A sort order that ignores stock and margin

By default, most engines sort results by raw text relevance, or worse, by catalog add date. Result: an out-of-stock product can show up first, while an available, more profitable item ends up on page three.

A sort order that factors in stock, margin, and actual relevance directly changes the conversion rate of search results pages — without touching the rest of the site.

3. Missing synonyms and business vocabulary

Your customers don't always speak your catalog's language. A shopper might search "jumper" where your product sheet says "sweater," or "trainers" where you wrote "sneakers." Without a synonym dictionary tailored to your business, each of these searches returns zero results — even though the product genuinely exists.

A search engine that returns "no results" for a product you have in stock isn't a technical detail — it's a lost sale, every single time it happens.

How to check where you stand

Three tests are enough to get a first read, directly on your own site:

  • Search for a product you know is in stock, with a deliberate typo
  • Search a generic term from your sector ("sweater," "shoes"...) and look at what comes up first
  • Search a common synonym of one of your flagship products rather than its exact name

If any of these three tests fails, that's a concrete signal — not a matter of opinion about design or UX, but a measurable problem costing you sales every day.
