Synonyms and industry jargon: when your customers' vocabulary breaks your search engine

The buyer searches "self-tapping screw." Your product sheet is filed under "sheet metal screw." Result: blank page, frustration, abandoned cart — even though the product exists.

In technical sectors, the same product can have several names depending on use, region, or buyer habit. Without a synonym dictionary tailored to your business, every vocabulary variation you didn't anticipate returns a blank result.

Why generic synonyms aren't enough

Some search engines ship with a general-purpose synonym dictionary (standard English, for instance). That's useful, but insufficient for business vocabulary: "sheet metal screw" and "self-tapping screw" aren't dictionary synonyms — they're synonyms specific to your industry, and only a dictionary built from your own business can connect them.

Building a synonym dictionary that doesn't backfire

The classic trap is creating overly broad associations that generate false positives. A concrete example: linking "stainless" to "stainless steel" makes sense, since a customer typing either should find the other. But too broadly linking every term containing "steel" to "stainless" would surface non-stainless steel products on a stainless search — a false positive that erodes trust in the engine.

Two types of rules to distinguish:

  • Bidirectional synonyms: both terms are genuinely interchangeable in either direction ("sweater" ↔ "jumper").
  • Unidirectional synonyms: one term should surface the other, but not the reverse — useful when one term is more generic than the other.
An overly permissive synonym dictionary is almost as much of a problem as an empty one: it surfaces results that have nothing to do with the search, and the buyer stops trusting it.

Finding synonym opportunities without guessing

The most reliable method isn't imagining synonyms upfront — it's analyzing the queries your visitors actually type that return zero results. These "zero-result searches," visible in most analytics tools or in the engine's own internal stats, directly reveal the vocabulary your customers use that your catalog doesn't yet recognize.

A regular review of this list, even a simple one (the month's 50 most frequent zero-result queries), is often enough to identify the synonyms that pay off the most, without needing to cover every possible term at once.
